# Anti-AI Writing Filter

Never use any word or phrase from this list in any article.

These words and phrases are the clearest signals of AI-generated content. They appear in Wikipedia's "Signs of AI writing" article, are flagged by AI-detection tools, and cause readers to immediately distrust the content. A single one of these phrases in an article is enough to destroy the credibility that the E-E-A-T signals are trying to build.

---

## Banned Words (single words)

- leverage (use: use, apply, take advantage of)
- delve (use: explore, dig into, examine, look at)
- tapestry (never appropriate in tool reviews)
- realm (use: space, area, field)
- landscape (use: space, market, field)
- ecosystem (unless referring to an actual software ecosystem — use sparingly)
- robust (use: strong, reliable, capable)
- comprehensive (use: thorough, detailed, complete)
- seamless / seamlessly (use: smooth, easy, without friction, without extra steps)
- cutting-edge (use: new, recent, modern)
- revolutionary / revolutionize (use: changes how X works)
- game-changer (say what specifically changes and why)
- transformative / transform (use: changes, improves)
- elevate (use: improve, lift, raise)
- unleash (never appropriate)
- supercharge (never appropriate)
- empower (use: lets you, gives you the ability to)
- facilitate (use: help, make easier, enable)
- foster (use: build, grow, encourage, support)
- navigate (use: move through, manage, handle, work with)
- crucial (use: important, key, critical — or be specific about why)
- vital (use: important, necessary — or be specific)
- innovative / innovation (use: new approach, new way of doing X)
- utilize (use: use — always)
- synergy (never appropriate in tool reviews)
- holistic (never appropriate)
- streamline (use: simplify, speed up, reduce steps)
- meticulous (use: careful, thorough, detailed)
- commendable (sounds like a school report)
- noteworthy (use: worth mentioning, stands out)
- invaluable (use: very useful, essential — or say why specifically)

## Banned Phrases

- "It's worth noting that..." (just say it)
- "In conclusion..." (use: The bottom line is / Final verdict)
- "In today's digital age..." (always cut — every article starts this way)
- "In today's fast-paced world..." (same)
- "In the ever-evolving world of..." (same)
- "In today's digital world..." (same)
- "Look no further..." (never use)
- "Look no further than..." (never use)
- "Without further ado..." (never use)
- "It goes without saying..." (never use — if it goes without saying, don't say it)
- "Needless to say..." (never use)
- "At the end of the day..." (use: Ultimately / In the end — or rewrite)
- "When all is said and done..." (never use)
- "The bottom line is..." (acceptable once, at end of verdict only)
- "Think of it as..." (use sparingly — only if the analogy is actually useful)
- "Gone are the days..." (never use)
- "A treasure trove of..." (never use)
- "A plethora of..." (use: many / a lot of / dozens of)
- "The world of [X]..." (cut the framing, start with the point)
- "On the other hand..." (fine occasionally, but overused — vary)
- "That being said..." (use: But / However / Still)
- "With that in mind..." (usually cut — the next sentence stands alone)
- "It is important to note..." (just say it)
- "It is worth mentioning..." (just say it)
- "Suffice it to say..." (never use)
- "As we navigate..." (cut navigate entirely)
- "As we delve into..." (cut delve entirely)
- "Let's dive in..." (cut — start the section)
- "Buckle up..." (never use)
- "Now more than ever..." (never use)
- "More than ever before..." (never use)
- "The power of [X]..." (be specific about what X does instead)
- "Unlock the power of..." (never use)
- "Harness the power of..." (never use)
- "Take your [X] to the next level..." (never use)
- "State-of-the-art..." (use: recent, modern, or name the specific feature)
- "Powerful tool" (say what it does powerfully)
- "Robust features" (say which features specifically)
- "Robust solution" (say what problem it solves and how)
- "Seamless integration" (say what it integrates with and how)
- "User-friendly" (say: easy to set up / takes 5 minutes / no learning curve needed)
- "Game-changing features" (name the features)

## Warning Phrases (use with extreme caution)

These are not always wrong but become AI slop when overused. Use at most once per article, only where genuinely apt:

- "In short..." (fine as a summary signal, but varies per para)
- "Ultimately..." (fine at verdict, not mid-article)
- "In other words..." (only if the clarification is genuinely needed)
- "For example..." (fine but vary with "For instance", "Specifically", "Take X:")
- "Keep in mind..." (fine occasionally — do not open paragraphs with it)

---

## How to Fix These Patterns

Instead of describing features with adjectives, describe what the feature **does**:
- WRONG: "ElevenLabs offers robust, seamless voice cloning."
- RIGHT: "ElevenLabs clones a voice from a 30-second sample. The output is indistinguishable from the original in back-to-back listening tests."

Instead of framing an article with a landscape observation, start with the reader's problem:
- WRONG: "In today's fast-paced content creation landscape, creators need tools that streamline their workflow."
- RIGHT: "Recording a podcast and having it ready to publish in under an hour used to require a full editing team. Now one tool handles it."
